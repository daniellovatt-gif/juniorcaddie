Junior Journey · Competition Day

The parent's guide to competition day.

First-time golf parents are often completely lost on the day — where to stand, when to be quiet, what's happening on the scorecard. This is the guide we wish someone had given us before Jacob's first competition.

Section 1

Before you arrive

Check the tee time and be at the club at least 30 minutes early — junior competitions almost always require registration, and marker cards or scorecards are often handed out on a first-come basis. Arriving late is one of the most stressful ways to start a round, for both of you.

If it's genuinely your child's first competition, it's worth calling the club or checking with the organiser beforehand so they know to expect a newcomer. Most junior organisers are used to this and are glad to help settle a first-timer in.

Good to know: most junior opens are run by volunteers or the club professional, not paid officials. A friendly, low-pressure attitude from you sets the tone for how your child experiences the whole day.
Section 2

Arriving and registration

Your child — not you — should register if at all possible. It's a small thing, but it starts the day with them taking ownership, which matters more than it seems. Help them find their name on the draw sheet, locate their tee time and starting hole, and point them towards the practice putting green if there's time.

This is also the moment to make sure they know who their playing partners are for the round — most junior competitions play in groups of two or three, often with juniors they haven't met before. That's normal, and part of the experience.

Section 3

Where to stand — and where not to

This is the question every new golf parent has and nobody quite explains. Here's the honest version:

  • You can watch. Walking the course at a distance is completely normal and welcomed at almost every junior event.
  • Stay back from the group. A good rule of thumb is far enough that you can't be heard offering advice, but close enough to see the shots. Many organisers ask parents to stay 30+ yards back, off to the side, not walking directly with the group.
  • Never walk inside the ropes or with the group on the fairway unless you're the appointed marshal or official for that group.
  • Don't coach during the round. No club advice, no line-reading, no reaction to shots beyond quiet encouragement. This is the single biggest thing juniors say they wish parents wouldn't do — and it can result in a penalty for outside assistance in some formats.
  • It's fine to skip a few holes and meet them further round, especially on a hot day or a long course. You don't need to walk all 18.
If you're ever unsure where you're allowed to be, ask the starter or organiser at registration. They would always rather tell you than have to ask you to move mid-round.
Section 4

What's actually happening on the scorecard

Most junior opens use one of a small number of formats — Stableford, Stroke Play, or occasionally Texas Scramble for team events. If you're not sure which, our Golf Formats guide explains each one simply, with a Stableford points calculator.

Juniors mark each other's cards, not their own — this is standard and part of the etiquette they're learning. If your child is playing with someone they don't know, it's normal for there to be a short pause after each hole while scores are agreed and written down. That's not a problem; it's just part of the pace of competition golf.

At the end of the round, both markers and the player usually need to sign the card before it's handed in. This matters — an unsigned or incorrectly totalled card can occasionally lead to disqualification, so it's worth a gentle reminder (to your child, not to the whole group) to check this before they leave the last green.

Section 5

Etiquette other parents will notice

  • Keep phone calls and loud conversation away from tees and greens
  • Applaud good shots from any junior in the group, not just your own child
  • Don't discuss another child's score or performance within earshot
  • If your child has a bad hole, resist the urge to react visibly — a calm face from you does more than you'd think
  • Thank the organiser or marshal before you leave — junior golf runs almost entirely on volunteers
Section 6

After the round

This is the part that shapes whether your child wants to do this again. Lead with something specific and positive before anything else — a good shot, a moment of composure, the fact they finished. Save any technical debrief, if there needs to be one at all, for another day.

Let your child tell you how the round went before you tell them how you thought it went. Often their version is more balanced than you'd expect — juniors are usually harder on themselves than any parent needs to be.

Worth remembering: the goal of a first competition isn't a good score. It's whether your child comes away wanting to play in another one.
Before you go

What to bring — checklist

Clubs, cleaned, with the right number for the format (check the club's rules)
Golf shoes and a spare pair of socks
Waterproofs — even if the forecast looks good
Sun cream, cap or visor, sunglasses
Water and snacks for a full round (2.5–4 hours)
A pencil (most clubs provide these, but it's worth having a spare)
Entry fee or confirmation, if not already paid online
Handicap card or CDH number, if required for entry
