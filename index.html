Golf jargon — plain English

Handicap
A number showing your child's level. Lower = better. Beginners start around 54. Single figures means county level.
Strokeplay / Medal
Count every shot over 18 holes. Lowest total wins. The most common competition format.
Stableford
Points per hole rather than counting every shot. More forgiving for beginners. Highest points total wins.
Order of Merit
A season-long county leaderboard. Points earned at junior opens across the year. Top juniors are recognised at the end of the season.
CDH / WHS Number
Your child's unique ID in the England Golf system. They get it when they register for a handicap at their club.

Q My child has just started golf lessons. Do they need their own clubs straight away? +
Stage 1 — First Swing

Not at all. Most junior coaching programmes will have clubs your child can borrow while they find their feet. The last thing you want to do is spend £150 on a starter set before you know whether golf is going to stick. Give it two or three months of lessons first. If they're still loving it and the coach thinks they're ready to play some holes, that's the moment to invest in a basic set. And when you do — buy second-hand. A quality used set of US Kids clubs will serve a beginner far better than a cheap new set from a supermarket.

Q What age should a child start golf? My daughter is five — is that too young? +
Stage 1 — First Swing

Five is absolutely not too young — some of the best junior golfers in the UK started at four or five. The key at this age is that it's entirely fun-led. Short sessions, no pressure, lots of positive reinforcement. US Kids Golf design their equipment specifically for children from age three upwards, and their whole philosophy is built around making the game enjoyable from the very first swing. If your daughter shows any interest at all, nurture it — but let her set the pace entirely.

Q How do I know what size clubs my child needs? Everyone seems to give different advice. +
Stage 2 — Finding the Game

The answer is simpler than most people make it sound: measure your child's height. Not their age — their height. The leading junior golf brand, US Kids Golf, bases their entire sizing system on height, and it's the right approach. A tall nine-year-old and a small twelve-year-old may well need the same length clubs. As a rough guide, children under 4ft need a starter set, 4ft to 4ft 6in need a 7-club set, and 4ft 6in to 5ft need a more complete set with irons and a hybrid. Our Club Finder tool gives you a precise recommendation based on your child's exact height — use that as your starting point.

Answered by JuniorCaddie

Q Is it worth buying new junior clubs or should I always buy second-hand? +
Stage 2 — Finding the Game

For most families at Stage 2, second-hand is almost always the smarter choice — and not just for cost reasons. Junior clubs are outgrown quickly. A well-looked-after set of used US Kids clubs bought for £80 will perform as well as a new set costing £200, and when your child grows out of them in 12-18 months, you'll be able to sell them on for close to what you paid. The exception is if your child is showing serious talent and your coach recommends a specific set for performance reasons — at that point the investment in new, properly fitted clubs can be justified. But for most juniors still finding their game, quality second-hand is the way to go.

Q How does my child get their first golf handicap in the UK? +
Stage 3 — Taking it to the Course

Getting a first handicap is one of the great milestones in a junior golfer's journey — and the process is more straightforward than it might seem. Your child needs to be a member of a golf club affiliated to England Golf, or sign up for an iGolf membership if they're not yet a club member. They then need to submit a minimum of three 18-hole scorecards — or the equivalent in 9-hole rounds — through their club's handicap system, usually Intelligentgolf. These rounds must be pre-declared as handicap rounds, played under the Rules of Golf, and signed by a playing partner. Once those three scores are in, the World Handicap System calculates an initial handicap index automatically. The whole process typically takes a few weeks from the first submitted round.

Q My child has just got their first handicap. What junior competitions should they enter first? +
Stage 3 — Taking it to the Course

Start close to home and keep the pressure low. Your child's own club junior competitions are the perfect first step — familiar course, familiar faces, low stakes. Once they've played a few of those and have a feel for competitive golf, junior open competitions at nearby clubs are the natural next step. These are one-day strokeplay events that welcome juniors with any handicap and are a brilliant introduction to competing against players from other clubs. Your county golf union will publish a calendar of junior opens — that's the list to bookmark. Resist the temptation to enter county championships or tour events too early. The goal at this stage is positive competitive experiences, not results.

Q My child is playing county level. Is it worth getting a custom club fitting? +
Stage 4 — Serious Junior

At county level, absolutely yes — and the sooner the better. Most junior golfers playing at this standard are using clubs that are either the wrong length, wrong lie angle, or wrong shaft flex for their swing. A proper fitting with a PGA professional who has experience fitting juniors — not just adults — can make a significant difference to ball striking consistency. The challenge is that juniors are still growing, so a fitting done now may need revisiting in 12-18 months. A good fitter will account for this and recommend specs that give some room for growth. Budget around £50-£75 for the fitting itself, separate from any equipment cost.

Q How do county golf squads work and how does my child get selected? +
Stage 4 — Serious Junior

Each county golf union runs its own junior squad programme, but the principles are broadly similar across England. Selection is based on handicap index and competitive performance in county events during the season. Most counties have an age-banded structure — under 14s, under 16s, and under 18s — with squads at development and performance levels. The best way to get noticed is consistent performance in your county's own junior open series and championships, which county coaches attend specifically to identify talent. Contact your county golf union directly to understand their specific selection criteria — the details vary significantly between counties.

Q My son has outgrown his junior clubs. When should he move to adult equipment? +
Stage 5 — The Transition

It's about height and physical development, not age. Most juniors transition to adult-length clubs when they reach around 5ft 5in to 5ft 9in, though a strong junior with good swing speed may benefit from adult shafts earlier. The tell-tale sign is when your child's current clubs feel too short and they're compensating in their swing — standing too upright, gripping down too far, or losing distance despite improving technique. Rather than replacing everything at once, consider starting with adult-length irons and keeping junior woods a little longer while the transition completes. A fitting at this stage is essential rather than optional — and our marketplace is a good place to sell the outgrown junior set to fund what comes next.
