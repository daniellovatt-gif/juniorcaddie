The Junior Golf Journey

Five stages.
One incredible journey.

Understanding which stage your child is at — and what they need at each one — is the most important thing a golf parent can know.

🎯 What this stage is really about

Stage 1 has one purpose and one purpose only: to make your child fall in love with golf. Not to produce a technically correct swing. Not to enter competitions. Not to prove they have talent. The sole measure of success at Stage 1 is whether your child wants to come back next week.

The brain of a young child is extraordinarily receptive to new movement patterns — but only when the learning is wrapped in play and enjoyment. The moment it feels like work, the moment pressure enters the room, the brain switches off. Junior golfers who fall in love with the game at Stage 1 are the ones who are still playing at eighteen. Those who are pushed too hard, too technically, too soon, are the ones who quietly stop going to lessons and find a reason not to go to the club.

At Stage 1, the coach's entire job is to make the child fall in love with the game. Technique follows passion — it never precedes it. A coach who spends 45 minutes correcting a six-year-old's grip is a coach who is killing the game in that child.

— PGA Junior Development Coach

🌱 How your child will develop

Progress at Stage 1 is rarely linear and almost never looks impressive from the outside. Your child will swing and miss repeatedly. They will show flashes of genuine contact followed by complete regression the following week. They will get bored and wander off. All of this is entirely normal.

What you are looking for is not technical improvement. You are looking for a growing enthusiasm for being at the golf course. You are looking for your child asking when they can go again. You are looking for them practising in the garden or asking questions about golf on the way home. These are the signs that Stage 1 is working.

👨‍🏫 What good coaching looks like

A skilled Stage 1 coach builds every session around games and targets rather than technical instruction. They hit at coloured cones, chip into hula hoops, score points in small competitions, play games that make the child laugh. The technical learning is embedded invisibly in the fun.

Sessions should be short — 30 to 45 minutes at most. A young child's concentration window is brief. Three enthusiastic 30-minute sessions are worth more than one exhausting two-hour lesson. If your child's coach is spending large portions of each lesson in technical correction and your child is showing boredom or reluctance — that is a warning sign.

💛
How to support your child at Stage 1

Ask one question after every session: "Did you enjoy it?" Not "Did you hit it well?" Not "What did the coach say?" Just: did you enjoy it. If the answer is yes, the session was a success, regardless of what happened technically.

Never repeat the coach's technical instructions on the drive home. This is the most common mistake golf parents make. The coach has a plan. Adding to it, contradicting it, or repeating it creates confusion and pressure. The drive home should be about what they enjoyed, not what they need to improve.

Resist comparing your child to others. Junior golf development is completely non-linear. A child who appears to make no progress at five may suddenly flourish at seven. The only meaningful measure at Stage 1 is enthusiasm.

Equipment at Stage 1
Keep it simple. Don't spend much.

Three clubs at most — a driver or fairway wood, a mid-iron, and a putter. Always size by height, never by age. US Kids Golf's height-based sizing is the most reliable guide.

Buy second-hand without hesitation. A quality pre-loved starter set serves a Stage 1 child just as well as anything new — and when they outgrow it in 12 months, you sell it on for close to what you paid.

🔥 What this stage is really about

Stage 2 is where the game takes hold. Your child has survived the initial dropout period — typically 12 to 18 months in — and made an internal decision that golf is theirs. They think about it between sessions. They practise in the garden. They ask questions about professional golfers. This intrinsic motivation is one of the most valuable things in junior development — and it is surprisingly fragile.

Technically, Stage 2 is the most important developmental window in a junior golfer's career. The movement patterns embedded between the ages of seven and eleven will form the foundation of a player's swing for life. A coach who gets Stage 2 right gives their pupil a foundation that makes everything easier at every subsequent stage. A coach who builds compensations into the swing at this stage creates problems that can take years to undo.

The child who learns to love chipping and putting at age eight will outscore the long hitter at age twelve every time. The short game is where junior golf is actually played — and where confidence is built or lost.

— PGA Junior Development Coach

📈 How your child will develop

At Stage 2, genuine technical development begins. Your child will develop a more consistent ball-striking pattern, better coordination between body rotation and arm swing, and a growing understanding of how different clubs produce different results. Their short game becomes increasingly important — a Stage 2 child who has a reliable putting stroke and a basic chip shot has a tool that lets them score on a real golf course.

Mentally, Stage 2 introduces managing frustration. At Stage 1, most children are so new that a bad shot is simply part of the experience. At Stage 2, expectations develop — and with expectations come frustration. Learning to absorb a bad shot, reset, and play the next one with full focus is one of the most important skills in golf, and it begins to be teachable here.

💛
How to support your child at Stage 2

The drive home from golf is sacred. Research on junior sport development consistently identifies the car journey home as the place where a child's enjoyment is most frequently undermined. Keep it positive. Ask what they enjoyed. Don't discuss what went wrong unless they bring it up.

Don't offer technical advice. Your child's coach has a plan. Well-intentioned swing tips from a parent — however accurate — contradict that plan and create confusion. If you have concerns about technical development, raise them with the coach directly. Not in the car.

Play with them. If you play golf, getting on the course with your child at Stage 2 is one of the greatest gifts you can give them. Keep it casual, fun, and let them set the pace. If you don't play, consider learning — not to become a competitor, but to share an experience that connects you for decades.

Equipment at Stage 2
This is when equipment quality starts to matter.

A properly fitted set — typically seven to nine clubs. Shaft length based on height, shaft flex almost always graphite. Lighter is better for a developing swing speed.

US Kids Golf dominate this category for good reason. Their height-based fitting system is genuinely reliable. A set at the right specification will serve your child significantly better than an adult set cut down — which produces poor strike patterns and builds bad habits.

🎯 What this stage is really about

Stage 3 is the moment the game becomes real. A first handicap is a rite of passage — the formal recognition that this child is a golfer, measured and recorded within the structure of the game. For many juniors it is the first time they have a number that reflects their ability in any sport, and the emotional weight of that should not be underestimated.

Competition introduces pressures that practice and casual golf never can. Your child must now perform under scrutiny — from other players, from the scoreboard, often from parents watching. They will experience the full emotional range that golf provides: the elation of a birdie in competition, the frustration of a card-wrecking hole. How they learn to process these experiences will shape them not just as golfers but as people.

The transition from practice to competition is the biggest in junior golf. A child can be technically excellent on the range and fall apart on the course. The difference is almost always mental — and at Stage 3 the mental game is enormously influenced by what the parents do and say.

— PGA Junior Development Coach

🗺️ Getting the competition balance right

One of the most common mistakes at Stage 3 is over-competing. A parent who enters their child in every available competition every weekend creates a schedule that leaves no time for practice, no time for rest, and no time to embed the technical changes their coach is working on. If every weekend is a competition, there is no time to develop a better game.

A well-structured Stage 3 season includes meaningful competition balanced with dedicated practice time, fun golf with friends and family, and genuine rest. Junior golf burnout at Stage 3 is real. Children who play 40+ competitive rounds a year at age 10 or 11 are significantly more likely to disengage from the game by their early teens.

💛
How to support your child at Stage 3

Your behaviour at competitions matters more than you know. Junior golf research consistently identifies parental behaviour during competition as one of the strongest predictors of whether a child continues to compete. Be quiet when your child is playing. React identically to a birdie and a bogey. Never discuss the scorecard during a round.

The debrief has a formula. Three positive observations before any area of improvement is discussed. Ask your child what they think first — they almost always know what went wrong. What they need from you is not analysis. It is stability.

Watch for signs of burnout. A child who is reluctant to go to golf, who complains of fatigue before competitions, who becomes emotional around competition days — these are warning signs. Talk to the coach. Consider reducing the schedule. The long-term relationship with the game is worth more than any short-term result.

Equipment at Stage 3
Quality and fitting both matter now.

A full set — driver, fairway wood or hybrid, irons through to sand wedge, putter. A proper club fitting with a PGA Professional experienced in junior fitting is now justified. Incorrect lie angles and shaft lengths at this level actively impede technique — the player compensates for the equipment.

Plan for the next resize before you need it. A rapidly growing Stage 3 junior with the wrong clubs for six months of the season is a false economy. Sell through JuniorCaddie promptly — demand for quality Stage 3 equipment is high.

🎯 What this stage is really about

Stage 4 is where junior golf becomes a serious pursuit. The child is training with intent, competing at county level or above, and investing significant time and energy in their development. For families, this stage brings new complexity — the competitive calendar is more demanding, the coaching is more specialised, and the emotional stakes are higher for everyone.

It is also the stage where the best coaches spend considerable time on something most families never discuss explicitly: identity. A junior at Stage 4 needs a healthy relationship with their performance — one where they understand they are not defined by their handicap, their county ranking, or their results. Players who tie their self-worth to their scores are fragile. Players who see golf as something they do rather than something they are — are resilient.

The players who make it from Stage 4 to elite amateur golf are rarely the most talented children at twelve. They are almost always the ones who stayed curious, stayed coachable, and stayed in love with the game when it was hard. Talent is the starting point. Character is the destination.

— PGA Junior Development Coach

🏛️ The county pathway

Stage 4 is when most juniors engage seriously with the county structure for the first time. County squads, county championships, inter-county matches — these are environments that no club competition can replicate. The team element of inter-county golf introduces collective purpose and responsibility that is enormously valuable for a young player's development.

Selection for county squads varies between counties. The best preparation is consistent performance in county-organised events — the JuniorCaddie competition finder lists these across England. County coaches attend these events specifically to identify talent.

How to support your child at Stage 4

This is the stage where parental involvement most often becomes counterproductive. The stakes feel higher and it is natural to feel more emotionally engaged in outcomes. But a Stage 4 junior who is being managed too closely by their parents — whose coaching decisions are second-guessed, whose results are discussed too intensely — is a junior who is playing golf for their parents rather than for themselves.

Your role is logistics and emotional stability. You get them to training and competitions. You manage the schedule so they have enough rest and variety in their life. You are the same person after a 68 as after an 84. You trust the coach and the process.

Watch for identity fusion. If your child's mood and self-esteem become tightly linked to their golf results, this is a warning sign. A bad round should not ruin a weekend. The goal is perspective — golf is what they do, not who they are.

Equipment at Stage 4
Proper fitting is non-negotiable at this level.

A Stage 4 player should have been fitted by a PGA Professional with junior fitting experience. The variables at this level — shaft flex, lie angle, loft, grip size — have a direct and measurable impact on ball-striking. Off-the-shelf equipment, however expensive, will not perform as well as properly fitted equipment.

Many Stage 4 players begin transitioning toward adult-specification equipment. This should be managed with coach and fitter — it is not simply a matter of switching at a certain age. The right moment depends on physical development, swing speed, and technical maturity.

🚀 What this stage is really about

The Transition is not a single moment — it is a gradual process that typically takes one to three years and touches every aspect of a junior golfer's game. Equipment transitions from junior to adult specification. Competition moves from junior events into adult amateur golf. The coaching relationship deepens as the player begins to take genuine ownership of their own development.

The most important thing a family can do at Stage 5 is have honest conversations about the pathway. What does this player want to do with golf? Is college golf in the UK or United States a possibility? Is a professional career a realistic ambition? These questions deserve honest, well-informed answers — not overoptimistic projections, and not dismissive caution.

The transition to adult golf is not a graduation — it is an introduction. The player who arrives with humility, curiosity, and good habits will learn and improve faster than one who believes the junior chapter has already proved everything.

— PGA Junior Development Coach

🎓 The pathway question

For families where college golf is a possibility — in the UK or the United States — Stage 5 is the time to gather information and begin making decisions. The UK university golf pathway has grown significantly, with BUCS golf offering competitive opportunities that have launched a number of professional careers.

The US college golf pathway requires early planning. NCAA recruiting timelines begin earlier than most families realise. The process of identifying suitable college programmes, building a player profile, and making initial contact with college coaches benefits enormously from starting at 15 or 16 rather than 17 or 18.

For players considering the professional game: the pathway from promising amateur to professional golfer is long, expensive, and uncertain. This is not a reason not to pursue the professional dream — but it is a reason to pursue it with clear eyes, a financial plan, and a fallback that protects the player's future regardless of the outcome on the course.

How to support your child at Stage 5

Your role changes fundamentally at Stage 5. The child who started with a plastic starter set is now a young adult making their own decisions. Your role has shifted from logistics and support to something closer to a trusted advisor — available when asked, quiet when not. The parents who navigate Stage 5 most successfully are those who resist planning their child's future for them and instead provide stability that allows a young golfer to make brave decisions on their own terms.

Have honest conversations about the pathway. What are their academic options? What does a golf career actually look like financially? What happens if it doesn't work out? These conversations, handled with care and honesty, are among the most important things a parent can do at this stage.

Celebrate what they have already achieved. Whatever happens next, a junior golfer who has reached Stage 5 has developed character, resilience, discipline, and social skills that will serve them for the rest of their life. They have played extraordinary courses and been part of a sport that values honesty above almost everything else. That is not a consolation prize. It is the point.

Equipment at Stage 5
Sell the junior set. Invest properly in the adult one.

The transition moment is when the outgrown junior set has its highest resale value. A complete, well-maintained set of Stage 4 junior clubs is in significant demand. List on JuniorCaddie promptly and use the proceeds to offset the cost of the adult set.

A proper custom fitting is essential at Stage 5 — with someone who understands the specific challenges of the junior-to-adult transition. Wedge specification, driver shaft selection, and putter fitting all deserve individual attention. This is the most significant equipment investment of the junior journey.
