US Kids Golf has been setting the benchmark for beginner junior clubs since 1997, and the seventh generation Ultralight — the UL7 — doesn't change what works. If I had to recommend one set to every family just starting out in junior golf, this is it. Not because it's the most sophisticated piece of engineering you can buy, but because it solves the most important problem in junior equipment: the clubs are the right weight, the right length and the right flex for a small child who is still learning to swing.

The philosophical difference that sets US Kids apart from almost every competitor is the insistence on sizing by height, not age. The UL7 comes in nine distinct sizes, each calibrated for a three-inch height window. A 39-inch set for a child under 3'3" and a 66-inch set for a junior approaching adult stature. This is the correct approach. Age is a rough proxy for height. Two 9-year-olds can be 15 centimetres apart and will need completely different clubs. US Kids understood this decades before the industry caught up.

The club heads are 10–30% lighter than adult equivalents, which matters more than any other specification at Stage 1 and 2. A child who cannot generate clubhead speed because the head is too heavy will develop compensatory swing habits that coaches spend years undoing. The lighter the head, the more naturally a child can swing through the ball and the more positive their early experiences will be. The vibrant colour-coded DV3 woods — each club a different colour — make it immediately obvious which club does what, which is particularly helpful at Stage 1 when understanding the set is part of the learning.

The honest limitation of the UL7 is longevity. Your child will outgrow these clubs in 12–18 months during the fast-growth years, and the performance ceiling becomes apparent once they reach Stage 3 and begin competing. The shaft technology and iron construction, while perfect for beginners, does not serve a junior who is developing real swing speed and competing regularly. That is not a criticism — it is the correct set for the stage it serves. It simply means planning ahead.

The Tour Series 6 is US Kids Golf's premium performance range — the sixth generation of a line that has been the equipment of choice at the US Kids Golf World Championship for years. Where the Ultralight serves beginners and intermediates, the TS6 is built exclusively for advanced and elite junior golfers who are competing seriously and generating real clubhead speed. US Kids' own guidance is clear: when a junior's driver swing speed reaches 90mph or above, it's time to move to adult equipment. The TS6 is the club for every stage before that point.

The engineering step up from the Ultralight is substantial. The TS6 driver uses a variable thickness cup face design to achieve maximum allowable USGA/R&A ball speed limits — the same principle found in premium adult drivers. It is 0.75" to 2" longer than previous Tour Series models and more than 20 grams lighter, which directly translates to faster swing speeds for developing players. There is also an adjustable weight system in the driver — a feature previously found only in adult performance equipment — allowing players to dial in their preferred trajectory and spin characteristics. This is not beginner equipment wearing junior clothes. It is genuinely advanced performance hardware.

The irons are precision-tuned with CNC-milled faces and grooves to USGA conformance limits. The wedge range — 52°, 56° and 60° — has unique grinds specifically designed for each loft, ensuring proper turf interaction for different short game shot types. The hybrid features a thin crown design and low CG for a steep, stopping ball flight into greens. The complete set is as sophisticated as anything in junior golf short of a junior-spec fitting of adult equipment.

Height-based sizing is retained across the TS6 range, with options from 51" through 66" — typically covering juniors aged 9 through to the point at which they transition to adult equipment. US Kids also makes an epTour range for truly elite competitors, but for the vast majority of Stage 3–5 junior golfers, the TS6 represents the performance ceiling before adult equipment becomes appropriate.

JuniorCaddie Verdict
US Kids' most advanced junior range. If your child is competing at county level or above, generating serious swing speed, and has outgrown the Ultralight — the TS6 is the right next step before the transition to adult equipment. Properly engineered performance equipment at a more accessible price point than the Prodi G.

The PING Prodi G is the most thoughtfully engineered junior golf equipment I have ever seen, and I have been coaching juniors for eighteen years. When PING decided to make junior clubs, they did not make a junior version of adult clubs. They applied their entire adult engineering philosophy — custom fitting, premium materials, the same technology that goes into clubs used by Rory McIlroy and Viktor Hovland — to the specific physical and developmental requirements of a junior golfer. The result is a category apart.

The 15° titanium driver with 460cc head is the most advanced junior driver on the market. The Dragonfly Technology redistributes crown weight to the sole, pushing the CG lower and producing a higher, more consistent launch for slower junior swing speeds. Crown Turbulators reduce aerodynamic drag during the downswing — technology from PING's G-series adult drivers — helping generate more clubhead speed. I have seen juniors add 15–20 yards off the tee switching from packaged junior sets to the Prodi G driver. That is not marketing language. That is what happens when you give a 13-year-old a titanium driver engineered for their swing speed.

The irons are cast 17-4 stainless steel — the same premium material as the PING G400 series. Heel-toe perimeter weighting for forgiveness. Lower CG than any adult iron. The complete set of ten clubs covers driver, 22° fairway wood, 28° hybrid, 6-iron through pitching wedge, two specialist wedges and a choice of blade or mallet putter.

The Prodi G's most commercially intelligent feature is the one-time free adjustment programme. Purchase five or more clubs in a single transaction and PING will — once — lengthen, re-shaft, re-weight and re-grip the clubs as your child grows. This transforms the price calculation entirely. A set that would otherwise be outgrown in two years becomes a set that grows with your child for four or five years. The effective annual cost drops dramatically.

PING's WebFit Junior tool is genuinely exceptional. You enter height, wrist-to-floor measurement, skill level, age and average driver distance, and the system recommends specific club lengths, shaft flex and set makeup. It includes a gapping analysis — you can add or remove clubs and see the effect on distance gaps in real time. No other junior manufacturer comes close to this level of fitting sophistication.

"PING boss John K Solheim said he believes if juniors are exposed to better equipment, they'll enjoy golf more. My son now wants to play every Monday night, is asking for more lessons, and has stolen my Masters towel." — Bunkered magazine

The honest question is whether your child is at the right stage. The Prodi G serves juniors roughly 4'5"–5'2" — typically 10–15 years old — who are competing regularly and whose interest in golf is established. I would never recommend these clubs to a parent whose child has played for six months and "seems to enjoy it." I would enthusiastically recommend them the moment your child plays their first county event and comes home wanting to play again.

The 400cc titanium driver with inverted cone technology is the headline specification, and it earns the attention. TaylorMade's inverted cone — which increases the flexible face area to boost ball speed across off-centre hits — is the same principle used in the Qi and Stealth adult drivers. Applied to a junior driver at appropriate weights and lengths, it genuinely produces more consistent distance for junior swing speeds. The Size 3 7-iron is 30% lighter than TaylorMade's adult P-Series irons — a meaningful engineering achievement that requires real commitment to getting the swing weights right at junior-appropriate weights.

My one honest criticism of the Team TaylorMade range is the sizing system. It is age-based rather than height-based, which is the correct metric. A tall 7-year-old should almost certainly be in the Size 2 set (ages 7–9) rather than the Size 1. Parents need to check the height specifications — 42–47 inches for Size 1, 48–53 for Size 2, 54–59 for Size 3 — and use those, not the age labels, to make their selection. TaylorMade knows the height ranges are what matter. So should you.

The QR code included with each set, unlocking coaching tips from Rory McIlroy, Tommy Fleetwood, Brooke Henderson and others, is a genuinely clever addition. Children at Stage 1 and 2 who see their favourite tour professionals demonstrating drills are more likely to engage with practice. The stain-resistant bag — a practical detail that every parent of a young child will understand immediately — is similarly thoughtful.

The best packaged junior set available today. Better quality than most parents expect at this price. A genuinely smart purchase for the Stage 1–3 junior whose interest is confirmed. The Callaway XJ irons deserve particular mention. They use a low-profile design with an ultra-wide sole that makes it dramatically easier to get the ball airborne — exactly what a Stage 2 or early Stage 3 junior needs. The sole also provides significant bounce, which is forgiving when young players make the slightly heavy, turf-heavy contact that is completely normal at this stage of development. Callaway's Ai Smart Face technology on the driver — the same computational design used in their Epic and Paradym adult lines — produces surprising ball speed consistency for off-centre strikes.

The XT Teen is the set I most often recommend to parents of 13–15 year olds who have outgrown their junior clubs and are not yet at the height or strength where adult equipment makes sense. Ten clubs, properly sized for 5'2"–5'9", with Callaway's adult-grade face technology scaled to junior shaft lengths. It bridges the gap between junior and adult golf equipment better than any other product on the market and is significantly more sophisticated than the packaged 7-iron-and-putter sets most parents resort to at this stage.

MKids is a UK brand that doesn't get the attention it deserves outside coaching circles. Their Junior Pro Half Set, aimed at 9–14 year olds, uses a colour-coded sizing system that covers the height range from junior (about 4'3") up through tall junior (about 5'2") in distinct colour tiers. The approach is correct — height first, age second — and the quality at the price point is genuinely impressive.

The MKids Pro range uses graphite shafts throughout, properly weight-matched to junior swing speeds, and the irons have a sole width designed for developing players still making contact that is not always perfectly centred. For a Stage 2 junior who is playing regularly but not yet competing, the MKids Pro sits comfortably above the Ben Sayers entry level and makes a strong case as an alternative to the US Kids Ultralight at a similar or slightly lower price point in the UK market.

The reason MKids doesn't appear in more mainstream guides is distribution — they're not as widely stocked as US Kids or TaylorMade in the UK. The Golf Shop Online carries them and is the most reliable UK source. Worth seeking out. The quality-to-price ratio is consistently good.

Ben Sayers M1i is the set I recommend when a parent says "we're not sure golf is going to stick and we don't want to spend much." At £99 for a complete set with bag, it is honest, functional junior equipment. It will not produce the launch angles or ball speeds of the US Kids Ultralight, and the sizing is less precise than height-based systems. But it gets a child on a course with clubs that are genuinely designed for them, not cut-down adult equipment.

There are two versions: ages 5–8 and ages 9–11. The age-based sizing is the weakest element — I'd always check the actual club lengths against your child's height before buying. But for a genuinely tentative first investment where the question is "will my child even want to keep playing?", the Ben Sayers M1i does its job. If the answer is yes after a season, you will be buying US Kids or TaylorMade. The Ben Sayers has served its purpose well.

Guidance
When to upgrade
The four signs your child needs new clubs — none of which are "their birthday is coming up."
They've grown out of the sizing range
The most important signal. If the club feels short and they're compensating by bending excessively at the waist or standing too close to the ball, the clubs are too short. Measure their height against the sizing chart again.
They're competing and the equipment is limiting them
When your child starts losing distance to peers with better equipment, or when a coach specifically recommends an upgrade for performance reasons — that's the right moment. Not before.
The clubs are visibly damaged
Bent shafts, cracked grips, loose heads — any of these affect performance meaningfully. Don't wait for a natural upgrade milestone. Replace damaged clubs immediately.
Their interest has moved to a new level
When your child starts asking about competitions, talking about their handicap going down, or asking their coach about technique rather than just playing — their equipment commitment should match their interest commitment.
Smart buying
Buying second-hand junior clubs
The most financially intelligent thing most golf families can do. Here's how to do it well.

Junior golf clubs are outgrown, not worn out. A well-maintained set of US Kids Ultralight clubs bought for £80–£100 second-hand will perform identically to a new set costing £200. This is not a compromise — it is the correct financial decision for Stage 1 and Stage 2 juniors who will outgrow their clubs in 12–18 months regardless of quality.

What to look for when buying second-hand: Check the shaft for bends or cracks at the hosel. Check the grip for wear — regripping costs £5–£8 per club and is worth doing. Check the club face for deep groove wear or damage. US Kids Golf in particular holds its quality well — the club heads are robust and the shafts rarely fail. Always ask for the child's height so you can verify sizing, not just their age.
